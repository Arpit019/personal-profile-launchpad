## The Danger of Waterfall Research

Spending 6 weeks doing massive, upfront user research before writing a single line of code is an outdated, waterfall metric. By the time your research is compiled into a 50-page PDF, the market has shifted, and your engineering team has been sitting idle.

Modern product teams rely on **Continuous Discovery Habits**, a framework popularized by Teresa Torres. It is the practice of building products *with* users continuously, rather than just building products *for* them based on past data.

![Continuous Discovery](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1552664730-d307ca884978?auto=format&fit=crop&q=80)

### The Product Trio

Continuous discovery requires the "Product Trio": the Product Manager, the Lead Designer, and the Lead Engineer. All three must be present during customer interviews. 

If only the PM talks to customers and then hands "requirements" to engineering, nuance is lost. When an engineer hears a customer's pain point firsthand, they often invent technical solutions the PM didn't even know were possible.

### Automating the Pipeline

The biggest excuse PMs use for not interviewing customers is that recruiting them is too hard. Elite teams automate this pipeline.

Set up an automated email campaign (using Intercom or Customer.io) that triggers when a user completes a specific "Core Action" in your app. The email should simply say: *"Hey, it looks like you're getting value out of the app. I'm the Product Manager, and I'd love to give you a $50 Amazon gift card for 15 minutes of your time to hear your thoughts."*

Include a Calendly link. When you wake up on Monday, your calendar will be automatically populated with user interviews. Never stop talking to customers; the moment you do, your product begins drifting from market reality.
